# Research: Prior Art Against the Five Victory Conditions

Synthesized 2026-07-06. For each victory condition: the best existing serious research, the most instructive real-world precedent, and what a unifying blueprint would still need to originate.

## 1. One country — open borders, free movement

**Best existing research:** A mature academic literature, and the efficiency case is strikingly uncontroversial among economists who study it, even though the policy conclusion is politically radioactive. John Kennan's NBER model ([w18307](https://www.nber.org/papers/w18307)) finds open borders would produce welfare gains "about the same as the gains from a growth miracle that more than doubles the income level" in developing countries — driven by the "place premium" (the same worker earns dramatically more just by being in a higher-productivity location). Michael Clemens' widely-cited review aggregates independent model estimates at 50-150% gains to gross world product, with Klein and Ventura's model at the high end (121.69%); even Clemens' own conservative estimate is 20-60%.
**Most instructive real-world case:** No full open-borders case study exists at nation-state scale. Closest analogs are regional free-movement zones (EU Schengen, the historical US interstate labor market). The literature is overwhelmingly counterfactual/modeled, not observed at the scale this condition needs.
**What a blueprint still needs to originate:** The economics establishes the size of the prize; it says almost nothing about the transition mechanism — sequencing, the political economy of displaced domestic low-wage workers, or institutional design for managing free movement at global scale without a global sovereign. This is the single biggest originality gap across all five conditions. Worth an honest treatment of the "epidemiological case" objection (that migration could transmit poor institutions) — the actual paper making this case concludes it "could form a logically coherent efficiency case... but on current evidence... dynamically efficient policy would not imply open borders but would imply relaxation of current restrictions."

## 2. One currency — no financial arbitrage between nations

**Best existing research:** Two traditions, neither resolving cleanly into "just do this." (1) Keynes' Bancor proposal for an International Clearing Union and its descendant, the IMF's Special Drawing Right (SDR, created 1967) — a basket-of-currencies unit of account, subject of renewed reform interest since 2008. (2) Optimum Currency Area theory (Mundell, McKinnon, Kenen) — the framework for when a currency union is a good idea: trade integration, labor mobility, business-cycle synchronization, fiscal coordination.
**Most instructive real-world case:** The Eurozone — a cautionary tale, not a triumphant one. Labor mobility across EU borders is roughly 8x lower than across US state lines (0.3% vs. 2.4% of population relocating annually), so asymmetric shocks (the 2010s Eurozone crisis) couldn't be absorbed the way the US absorbs regional downturns.
**What a blueprint still needs to originate:** The least "assemble, don't invent" runway of the five. SDR proposals are reserve-asset reforms for central banks, not a retail single currency for a unified world economy. Key synthesis finding, not present in any single source: the five conditions likely have a real dependency order — open borders may need to substantially precede a shared currency, not run in parallel.

## 3. Food security for every human being

**Best existing research:** SDG 2.1 ("end hunger... all year round," 2030 deadline) is the clearest existing global-consensus target. Tracking data is sober: ~733 million people hungry in 2023 (1 in 11 globally, 1 in 5 in Africa), 2.33 billion facing moderate-to-severe food insecurity — a 15-year regression to 2008-2009 levels, flat for three straight years. ~582 million projected still undernourished in 2030 if trends hold.
**Most instructive real-world case:** No single national case dominates. The UN's own framing treats this as a distribution and affordability problem (71.5% of low-income-country populations can't afford a nutritious diet even where food exists), not a scarcity problem.
**What a blueprint still needs to originate:** The target and diagnosis are already assembled by the UN system. Missing: connecting food security's stalled progress to the open-borders literature — labor mobility itself is a hunger-reduction lever nobody in the SDG 2.1 literature models.

## 4. Housing security for every human being

**Best existing research:** SDG 11.1 sets a global target, but unlike food, housing has a proven national-scale intervention model, not just a target.
**Most instructive real-world case:** Finland's Housing First policy (national, since 2008, pioneered by the Y-Foundation) — house people unconditionally first, rather than requiring sobriety/treatment/employment milestones before housing. Hostel/boarding-house populations down 76% between 2008-2017; Finland is one of the only European countries where homelessness has actually declined. The implementation model matters as much as the policy idea: ARA (national housing finance body) subsidizing homes, NGOs and municipalities coordinating directly, 15+ years of sustained non-partisan commitment.
**What a blueprint still needs to originate:** Finland is small, wealthy, high-trust, already low-inequality. The real synthesis work is the honest case for what Housing First requires to scale to poor, high-inequality, low-state-capacity contexts where most of the global unhoused population actually lives.

## 5. Education without gatekeepers

**Best existing research:** The thinnest of the five — only SDG 4.1 surfaced directly ("all girls and boys complete free, equitable and quality primary and secondary education," 2030 deadline). A real coverage gap in the research itself, not just a hard problem.
**Most instructive real-world case:** None surfaced. Needs a dedicated follow-up search (MOOCs/open courseware, Khan Academy-style access models, specific national free-university systems).
**What a blueprint still needs to originate:** Almost everything. "Without gatekeepers" is sharper than the SDG language — SDG 4.1 is about curriculum completion, not about removing credentialing/access gatekeepers (accreditation, admissions, cost barriers to elite institutions), which is closer to what this condition actually means. Needs its own dedicated research pass before the blueprint can responsibly claim coverage.

## Cross-cutting: the poverty roadmap

UN Special Rapporteur on extreme poverty Olivier De Schutter spent 18 months building "A Roadmap for Eradicating Poverty Beyond Growth," presented to the UN Human Rights Council's 62nd session (2026) — explicitly framed as expanding the policy toolkit beyond growth-based poverty reduction. This is the single most structurally relevant precedent found: an actual attempt, backed by UN mandate, at roadmap-level synthesis across poverty-adjacent domains — closer to what this book is attempting than any single-domain source. The 2025 SDG progress report's own verdict on the 2030 Agenda: "improved millions of lives" but off-track, with conflict, climate, inequality, and debt servicing named as compounding headwinds across every domain, not just one.
