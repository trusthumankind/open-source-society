# One: Open Borders, Free Movement

A Bolivian worker with the same education, the same experience, the same skills, and the same work ethic earns 2.7 times more simply by doing that work in the United States instead of Bolivia. For a worker from Nigeria, the multiple is 8.4. Economists Michael Clemens, Claudio Montenegro, and Lant Pritchett measured this directly — not by comparing different people, but by comparing the predicted wages of *observably identical* people, matched on country of origin, education, experience, sex, and whether they live in a city or the countryside. The only variable left is which side of a line they're standing on. The average wage gap for a male migrant moving from a developing country to the US: $15,400 a year. Clemens, Montenegro, and Pritchett call this one of the largest remaining price distortions in any global market — larger, in most cases, than the wage discrimination measured by race or gender within a single country's labor market. A border isn't a formality. It's the single biggest determinant of what your labor is worth.

In 2024, a Wisconsin Examiner reporter traveled to the US-Mexico border and met some of the people on the other side of that number. One of them, according to the report, was a 42-year-old former bus driver from Oaxaca, migrating in part to help pay for his two daughters' education — the kind of ordinary, specific reason that a $15,400 statistic can describe but can't make you feel. He is one person. The research says there are hundreds of millions of people in his position: identical in every way that should matter, standing on the wrong side of a line that decides what they're allowed to earn.

The aggregate version of that same finding is almost as striking as the individual one. John Kennan's economic model of full open borders finds welfare gains "about the same as the gains from a growth miracle that more than doubles the income level" for people in developing countries. Independent economic models, built by different researchers using different methods, converge on estimates of 50 to 150 percent gains to gross world product from removing barriers to global labor movement — even Michael Clemens' own conservative estimate lands at 20 to 60 percent. Economists who study this do not seriously dispute that the efficiency case is enormous. What they dispute is what happens to the people already standing on the right side of the line when it's redrawn — and that disagreement, not the size of the prize, is the actual argument.

No nation-state has ever tried full open borders, so there's no completed case study to point to — the research here is overwhelmingly modeled, not observed. The closest real-world analog is Schengen, Europe's passport-free zone, which shows regional free movement working at meaningful scale without producing the collapse critics predict. It doesn't show what happens at the scale this book is asking about: not a regional zone opting in, but a genuinely global one.

The strongest serious objection isn't economic — it's what researchers call the "epidemiological case": the worry that open migration would import the poor institutions and low productivity of a migrant's origin country into the destination, and cancel out the efficiency gains the models predict. It's a real question, not a strawman, and the paper that examines it most directly concludes it "could form a logically coherent efficiency case against open borders in general equilibrium" — but that "on current evidence about the magnitudes of these parameters, dynamically efficient policy would not imply open borders but would imply relaxation of current restrictions." Translated: the objection is coherent in theory, and doesn't survive contact with the actual data we have.

What none of this research does is tell you *how*. The economics establishes the size of the prize and rules out the strongest objection to claiming it. It says almost nothing about sequencing, about the political reality of workers displaced by newly arrived competition, or about what institution — since there is no world government — would actually manage free movement at global scale. That gap is not a footnote. It is the largest piece of original work this book's blueprint has to do, for any of its five conditions.
