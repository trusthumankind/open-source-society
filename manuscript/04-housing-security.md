# Four: Housing Security, No Homelessness as a Systemic Condition

Rita Lahtinen spent seven years in temporary shelters in Helsinki. She had run a pub, sold the business in 2002, and fell into a tax debt she couldn't climb out of — a downward spiral that ended, as it does for hundreds of thousands of people every year, without a home. In April 2019, at 54, she moved into her own one-room flat, arranged through the Finnish NGO VVA. "It's changed my life," she told a reporter that September. Not because the debt vanished, or because she'd proven herself ready. Because someone gave her a door she could close before asking anything else of her. "It is hard to do any kind of demanding work if you are living in a shelter," she said. "This has been like a springboard for me."

That sentence is the entire argument of Finland's national housing policy, and the reason it works where almost every other country's approach doesn't. Since 2008, Finland has run its homelessness response on a principle called Housing First: give someone a home unconditionally, before sobriety, before treatment, before proving readiness — not after. Every other country still running the older model, sometimes called "staircase" housing, asks people to earn each step: shelter, then transitional housing, then supervised housing, then, maybe, a home of their own, contingent on meeting requirements at each stage. Finland inverted the sequence, on the theory that stability is the precondition for recovery, not the reward for it.

The results are the clearest, least ambiguous win in this book's entire five-condition survey. Finland's hostel and boarding-house population fell 76 percent between 2008 and 2017. It is one of the only countries in Europe where homelessness has gone down, not up, over the same stretch where most of the continent's numbers worsened. The policy has an implementation model behind it, not just an idea: ARA, a national housing finance and development agency, subsidizes the actual housing stock; NGOs like VVA and municipalities coordinate delivery directly; and the commitment has held for more than fifteen years across changes in government, which is itself part of why it worked — a program reversed every few years by a new administration never gets the chance to prove out.

The United Nations set the closest thing to a global version of this goal in Sustainable Development Goal 11.1: "adequate, safe and affordable housing" for everyone, with slums upgraded, by 2030. But housing is unusual among this book's five conditions in a specific way. Food security and open borders both have global targets and a stalled or thin track record of actually hitting them. Housing has the target and a fifteen-year, national-scale, still-running proof that the underlying approach works — not a model, not a projection, an actual country that tried it and measured the result.

Which makes the honest question sharper, not softer: does Rita Lahtinen's Finland generalize? Finland is small — about 5.6 million people. It is wealthy, high-trust, and already comparatively low-inequality before Housing First ever started. The core research answers "does unconditional housing work" about as conclusively as a single national case study can. It says almost nothing about whether the same model survives contact with a poor, high-inequality, low-state-capacity country — which describes most of the more than 1.12 billion people worldwide who, by UN-Habitat's own count, live in slums or informal settlements, out of a broader 2.8 billion who lack access to adequate housing, secure land, or basic water and sanitation altogether. ARA's subsidy model presumes a functioning national housing-finance institution and a tax base able to fund it. Most of the countries carrying the largest share of the global housing gap have neither.

There is existing research connecting migration and housing, but it runs in the opposite direction from what this book needs. US research finds immigrants make up over 30 percent of the construction workforce, and that reduced immigration measurably tightens housing supply and raises prices in the destination country — the Cato Institute estimates mass deportation could erase nearly a trillion dollars in US housing wealth by removing that labor. That is a real, well-documented effect, but it's about migration's effect on where people arrive, not about what would help house the billions of people in the countries people leave. Nobody in that literature is asking whether the place-premium economics from Section One — the same wage gap that makes migration valuable to an individual — has anything to say about financing or staffing housing at Finland's scale back where the housing gap is actually largest. That narrower, unasked question, not "does Housing First work," which Finland already answered, is the piece of the blueprint this book still has to originate.
