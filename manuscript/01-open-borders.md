# One: Open Borders, Free Movement

A Bolivian worker with the same education, the same experience, the same skills, and the same work ethic earns 2.7 times more simply by doing that work in the United States instead of Bolivia. For a worker from Nigeria, the multiple is 8.4. Economists Michael Clemens, Claudio Montenegro, and Lant Pritchett measured this directly — not by comparing different people, but by comparing the predicted wages of *observably identical* people, matched on country of origin, education, experience, sex, and whether they live in a city or the countryside. The only variable left is which side of a line they're standing on. The average wage gap for a male migrant moving from a developing country to the US: $15,400 a year — and that figure is already adjusted for purchasing power, meaning it accounts for the fact that a dollar buys less in a high-cost country than a low-cost one. The higher cost of living in the destination doesn't explain away the gap; the researchers built the cost-of-living difference into the number before they published it. Clemens, Montenegro, and Pritchett call this one of the largest remaining price distortions in any global market — larger, in most cases, than the wage discrimination measured by race or gender within a single country's labor market. A border isn't a formality. It's the single biggest determinant of what your labor is worth.

In 2024, a Wisconsin Examiner reporter traveled to the US-Mexico border and met some of the people on the other side of that line. One of them, according to the report, was a 42-year-old former bus driver from Oaxaca, migrating in part to help pay for his two daughters' education — the kind of ordinary, specific reason that a $15,400 statistic can describe but can't make you feel. He is one person. The research says there are hundreds of millions of people in his position: identical in every way that should matter, standing on the wrong side of a line that decides what they're allowed to earn.

The aggregate version of that same finding is almost as striking as the individual one. Economists use the term "welfare gains" to mean something specific and much broader than its everyday meaning — not government assistance, but the total increase in what people are actually able to produce, earn, and consume once a barrier to efficient work is removed. John Kennan's economic model of full open borders finds gains on that scale "about the same as the gains from a growth miracle that more than doubles the income level" for people in developing countries. Independent economic models, built by different researchers using different methods, converge on estimates of 50 to 150 percent gains to gross world product from removing barriers to global labor movement — even Michael Clemens' own conservative estimate lands at 20 to 60 percent. Economists who study this do not seriously dispute that the efficiency case is enormous. What they dispute is what happens to the people already standing on the right side of the line when it's redrawn — and that disagreement, not the size of the prize, is the actual argument.

No nation-state has ever tried full open borders, so there's no completed case study to point to — the research here is overwhelmingly modeled, not observed. The closest real-world analog is the European Union's Schengen Area, where 29 countries agreed to drop their internal border checks and let roughly 450 million people move, live, and work across each other's territory without a visa. It isn't a perfect match for "the EU": a few EU members stay outside Schengen, and a few Schengen countries aren't in the EU at all. What matters for this book is simpler — it's the largest, longest-running proof that sovereign nations can open their borders to each other at real scale without the collapse critics predict. What it doesn't show is what happens at the scale this book is asking about: not a regional bloc of neighbors opting in, but a genuinely global, unconditional version of the same idea.

The strongest serious objection isn't economic — it's what researchers call the "epidemiological case": the worry that open migration would import the poor institutions and low productivity of a migrant's origin country into the destination, canceling out the efficiency gains the models predict. It's a real question, not a strawman. The paper that examines it most directly builds a model of what economists call "general equilibrium" — meaning it doesn't just track one effect in isolation, but simulates how migration, wages, and institutions all adjust together, the way they actually would in the real world. Even inside that fuller model, the paper's own conclusion is that the risk "could form a logically coherent efficiency case against open borders" in theory — but when the model is run using the actual, measured size of that risk rather than a worst-case guess, "dynamically efficient policy would not imply open borders but would imply relaxation of current restrictions." In plain terms: the objection survives as a logical possibility, but not as a reason to keep current restrictions in place, once you replace the worst-case assumption with the real data on how much institutions actually transfer across borders.

What none of this research does is tell you *how*. The economics establishes the size of the prize and rules out the strongest objection to claiming it. It says almost nothing about sequencing, about the political reality of workers displaced by newly arrived competition, or about what institution — since there is no world government — would actually manage free movement at global scale. That gap is not a footnote. It is the largest piece of original work this book's blueprint has to do, for any of its five conditions.
