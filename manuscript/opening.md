# Opening: One Problem, Not Five

In 2010, the Eurozone did something no group of sovereign nations had done before: it shared a currency without sharing a country. By that year, sixteen nations — Greece, Germany, Ireland, and thirteen others — had given up their own money and adopted one, betting that a common currency would pull them into a common economy.

Then Greece's economy collapsed, and the rest of the Eurozone found out what they'd actually built.

A country that controls its own currency can respond to a local recession by letting its money lose value — exports get cheaper, the economy rebalances, people keep their jobs. Greece couldn't do that anymore. It had given up that lever the day it adopted the euro. What should have happened next, according to the economic theory the Eurozone was supposedly built on, is that Greek workers would move to where the jobs were — Germany, the Netherlands, wherever demand existed — the same way an unemployed worker in Michigan can move to Texas without a visa, a border, or a currency exchange. That's what makes a currency union work: when money stops flowing to where the work is, people have to be free to flow there instead.

Except Europeans don't move like that. In any given year, about 0.3% of the EU's population relocates across a national border for work. In the United States, the equivalent number is roughly 2.4%. Eight times higher. The European Union had built half a currency union — the money half — without building the other half: a genuinely open labor market where people move as freely as capital does. Greece couldn't devalue its way out of the crisis, and Greek workers couldn't move their way out of it either. Unemployment climbed from under 10% in 2009 to a peak near 28% in 2013, and stayed in double digits for most of the following decade.

This is not a story about currency policy. It's a story about what happens when you solve one piece of an interlocking system and leave the rest in place.

---

That's the argument of this book, and it's worth stating plainly before anything else: the five conditions this book covers — open borders, a shared currency, food security, housing security, education without gatekeepers — are not five separate problems competing for your attention and your outrage. They are five faces of a single, connected system, and treating them as a checklist to work through one at a time is exactly the mistake the Eurozone made with just two of them.

The evidence for this isn't a metaphor. It's in the research. The clearest single precedent for what this book is trying to do — a unified diagnosis instead of a domain-by-domain report — comes from Olivier De Schutter, the United Nations Special Rapporteur on extreme poverty, who spent eighteen months building "A Roadmap for Eradicating Poverty Beyond Growth," presented to the UN Human Rights Council in 2026. His mandate wasn't housing, or hunger, or migration specifically. It was poverty itself, and his conclusion was that poverty can't be reduced domain by domain because it isn't caused domain by domain. The UN's own 2025 progress report on its Sustainable Development Goals says the same thing from a different angle: the world is behind on nearly every target it set for 2030, and the reasons named for the shortfall — conflict, climate shocks, rising inequality, debt servicing costs — are not specific to hunger, or housing, or education. They compound across all of them simultaneously.

You can see the compounding directly in two of this book's own five conditions. The research on open borders finds a genuine, serious economic literature — independent models converging on estimates of 50 to 150 percent gains to gross world product from free global labor movement, gains on the scale of "a growth miracle that more than doubles income" for people currently locked out of higher-productivity places by nothing but a border. That same literature has essentially nothing to say about hunger. The research on food security, separately, treats hunger as a distribution and affordability problem: hundreds of millions of people live within reach of enough food and still can't afford it. Nobody has connected these two facts, but they are the same fact wearing two different names. A hungry person in a low-wage, low-opportunity place and an unemployed person locked out of a high-wage, high-opportunity place by a border are, disproportionately, the same person. The economics literature that would prove this doesn't exist yet, because nobody who studies migration is asking about hunger, and nobody who studies hunger is asking about migration.

That gap — the space between five bodies of serious, credible, uncoordinated research that never talk to each other — is where this book lives. Not inventing new economics. Not new politics. The work is connection: taking five real, rigorous, already-serious answers to five real questions, and doing the thing none of their authors set out to do, which is showing that they were never five questions in the first place.

The Eurozone learned this the hard way, with one condition out of five, and spent a decade paying for it. The rest of this book is the case for learning it on purpose, with all five, before the cost gets paid again.
