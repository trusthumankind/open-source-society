# Three: Food Security for Every Human Being

In 2014, Brazil disappeared from the United Nations hunger map.

Not because someone stopped counting. Because for the first time in the agency's tracking, the share of Brazil's population going undernourished had fallen far enough — the country's own undernourishment rate had dropped by more than 80 percent in a decade — that the UN's Food and Agriculture Organization no longer classified it as a country with a hunger problem. This followed a program called Fome Zero, Zero Hunger, launched in 2003 as the flagship policy of a new government, built on cash transfers to poor families, support for small farmers, and school meal programs reaching tens of millions of children. Childhood malnutrition fell 31 percent in the program's first decade. The number of Brazilians living with hunger dropped from roughly 22.8 million in 1992 to 13.6 million by 2012. This is not a modeled projection or an aggregated global estimate. It happened, in one country, and was documented well enough that an international body changed its own map because of it.

Then Brazil came back onto the hunger map. Across the years that followed — under one government that weakened parts of the social safety net Fome Zero had built, and the next one, which dismantled the national food security council entirely and cut further — the programs that had done the work were rolled back, and hunger in Brazil rose again. Millions of people who had been lifted out of it fell back in, and the country returned to the UN's hunger map between 2018 and 2020. In 2023, a new administration made ending hunger its top priority again, and by the time the UN's 2025 tracking report was published, Brazil's undernourishment rate had dropped back below the threshold and the country came off the hunger map a second time.

The shape of that story matters as much as the ending. Hunger in Brazil was not solved and it was not fated. It was built, by policy, over a decade. It was dismantled, by different policy, over roughly four years. And it was rebuilt. The same country did all three, with the same population, the same land, the same resources, entirely as a function of what its government chose to fund and protect. If hunger were primarily a scarcity problem — not enough food, not enough farmland, not enough money — a country couldn't cross the threshold twice in one generation just by changing what it decided to prioritize. It isn't a scarcity problem. It's a distribution and affordability problem, and Brazil is the clearest single proof that exists of both halves of that claim: it is solvable, and it does not stay solved on its own.

That's what makes Brazil useful for a book with global ambitions and no illusions about how hard this is. It isn't a story about a country that got lucky with rainfall or geography. It's a story about what a government can build and what a different government can tear down, inside the length of a single political generation.

The global numbers show why this matters at a scale far beyond one country. The United Nations' own hunger target, Sustainable Development Goal 2.1, commits every signatory nation to ending hunger and guaranteeing safe, sufficient food "all year round" by 2030 — a deadline set in 2015. As of the most recent tracking, roughly 733 million people went hungry in 2023, about one in eleven people worldwide and one in five in Africa. A further 2.33 billion people faced moderate to severe food insecurity — not necessarily hunger every day, but real, chronic uncertainty about whether there would be enough. That 733 million figure is 152 million higher than in 2019, a fifteen-year regression, essentially unchanged for three straight years running. If the current trajectory holds, the UN's own projection is that roughly 582 million people will still be chronically undernourished in 2030 — the year the world promised itself this problem would be over.

The UN's own diagnosis of why progress stalled matches what Brazil's reversal shows directly: this is not primarily about food existing. It's about who can afford it. In the lowest-income countries, an estimated 71.5 percent of the population cannot afford a nutritionally adequate diet, even in places where that diet is physically available on a shelf somewhere nearby. Conflict, climate shocks, and food-price inflation are named as the drivers pushing the number in the wrong direction — the same compounding forces the Opening chapter traced across every one of this book's five conditions, not unique to this one.

The Opening already named the shape of a gap between the open-borders and food-security literatures. Checking that connection directly, rather than assuming it, turns up something more specific and better documented than the Opening's version suggested: the link isn't hypothetical, it's remittances. Migrants who leave a low-opportunity country for a higher-wage one routinely send part of that income home, and that income is one of the best-studied buffers against household food insecurity in the countries research on this actually covers — independent studies in Mexico, Nigeria, Sri Lanka, and Ethiopia each find remittance-receiving households eating more, more often, and with more dietary variety than otherwise-similar households that don't receive them. At the scale of the whole system, officially recorded remittances to low- and middle-income countries reached roughly $656 billion in 2023 — more than foreign direct investment and official development aid to those countries combined. Fome Zero worked by moving resources directly to people who couldn't afford what already existed on the shelf. Remittances are millions of individual households already running a smaller, unplanned, migration-dependent version of the same mechanism, entirely because someone crossed a border and a border only lets that flow of resources back through a narrow, restricted channel. What the existing research doesn't do is connect this to the open-borders literature's own claim from Section One — that current restrictions themselves are the inefficiency, and remittances are what leaks through despite them, not because of an open system designed to let it flow. Nobody has modeled what removing that restriction, rather than working around it, would do to the $656 billion baseline. That's the real, unclaimed piece of the roadmap this book exists to assemble.
