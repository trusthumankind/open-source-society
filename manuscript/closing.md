# Closing: The Roadmap Itself

Five sections of this book each ended the same way: naming the specific, unclaimed piece of work that existing research doesn't do. Not five separate footnotes. Put next to each other, they form the beginning of an actual sequence — the thing every domain-specific literature this book draws on stops short of, because no single one of those literatures was ever trying to answer "in what order."

One dependency is already confirmed, not inferred. Section Two showed it directly: the Eurozone built a shared currency in 2010 without the labor mobility a currency union actually needs to survive a shock, and Greece paid for the gap in a decade of unemployment. Bretton Woods showed the same order failing a different way in 1944, when the one serious attempt at a global currency was rejected specifically because it would have constrained the world's dominant economic power before that power had any reason to accept the constraint. Both cases point the same direction: a shared currency is not a condition to build early. It depends on open, working labor mobility already being substantially in place — which puts open borders ahead of it in any real sequence, not beside it.

The other four conditions don't line up as cleanly, and pretending they do would be exactly the kind of overclaim this book has spent five sections catching and correcting in itself. Two of them actually pull in opposite directions from the same underlying mechanism, and that tension is worth stating plainly rather than smoothing over.

Food security's fastest working lever, identified in Section Three, is remittances — money migrants already send home, moving through borders that are restricted, not open, and still totaling roughly $656 billion a year to low- and middle-income countries. That channel gets stronger, not weaker, the more open borders become. Housing's evidence, from Section Four, runs the other way: immigrant labor makes up over 30 percent of the US construction workforce, and reduced immigration measurably tightens housing supply and raises prices in the country people arrive in. More open borders plausibly help food security in the countries people leave while straining housing in the countries people arrive in, through the same underlying flow of people. Neither section's research was built to reconcile that tension with the other, because neither literature was ever asked to. This book can name the tension. It cannot yet resolve it, and saying so directly is more honest than a roadmap that quietly assumes every condition helps every other one.

Housing has a further complication borders and currency don't: Finland's proof depends on a functioning national housing-finance institution and a tax base able to fund it, conditions that exist mostly independent of how open a country's borders are or what currency it uses. A poor, low-state-capacity country could open its borders and adopt currency union in exactly the right order this book argues for, and still lack the institutional foundation Housing First requires. State capacity isn't a sixth victory condition this book left out. It's closer to a precondition running underneath at least two of the five, visible only once you try to sequence them against each other instead of describing each one alone.

Education without gatekeepers can't be placed in this sequence with any honesty yet, and that's a different kind of gap than the other four. Currency's gap is a design problem — nobody has built the thing. Borders' gap is an institutional problem — nobody has built the manager. Food and housing's gap is a connective problem — the research exists but hasn't been asked the right cross-condition question. Education's gap is more basic: there is no Finland-scale national case to even reason from, and the one real individual story this book found sits next to a statistic showing how rarely that story generalizes. A sequence needs at least two data points to infer an order from. This condition doesn't have them yet. Placing it anywhere in this roadmap — first, last, parallel to the others — would be inventing a claim this book's own research doesn't support, the exact move every other section in this book exists to catch.

So the honest roadmap, as far as the evidence assembled here actually supports one: open borders first, because currency depends on it and food security already runs partly through it, informally, at a scale ($656 billion a year) large enough to prove the mechanism works even while restricted. Currency second, once labor mobility is substantially real rather than merely legal — the distinction Section Two spent its whole argument establishing. Housing on a mostly separate track, gated less by the border-currency sequence than by state capacity, and genuinely strained rather than helped by the same migration that helps food security, a tension this book can name but not yet resolve. Education last not because it matters least, but because it is the one condition where the honest answer is that the research to sequence it against the others doesn't exist yet — which is itself the clearest single finding in this entire roadmap. Five domains of serious, credible, uncoordinated research, brought into the same room for the first time, agree on more than any of them expected and still leave real, named gaps between them. That is what assembling five separate answers into one roadmap actually looks like. Not a clean plan. A precise map of what's confirmed, what's in tension, and what hasn't been asked yet.
