# Five: Education Without Gatekeepers

Tony Byrd worked the coffee shop at IBM's Research Triangle Park campus for seven years after high school. He was good at it — good enough that the executives and engineers who came through his line every morning noticed, and one of them, a software engineer named David Green, started asking him questions about his interest in coding. Green began tutoring him informally, on the side, the way a regular customer might strike up a habit rather than a job offer. When IBM opened its apprenticeship program — a paid, twelve-month course for people without a four-year degree, teaching JavaScript, Python, and C# from the ground up — Green pushed him to apply, and stayed on as his mentor through the program itself, keeping him focused on finishing it. Byrd graduated in August 2019 and joined IBM's Cloud projects team as a software engineer. He now earns roughly three times his barista wage — enough, he's said, to start saving for his two kids' college educations, an opportunity he didn't have himself.

Byrd's story is the promise of what's usually called skills-based hiring: strip away the degree requirement, evaluate what someone can actually do, and let ability move people into roles a diploma used to gate. It's a real, replicable model — IBM alone has hired thousands of people through programs like it, with up to 15 percent of its recent US hires arriving without a traditional four-year degree. Apple, Google, Walmart, Tyson Foods, and dozens of other major employers have made the same public commitment: stop requiring degrees for roles that don't actually need one.

Here's the honest number behind that commitment. A 2024 Harvard Business School and Burning Glass Institute study, led by economist Joseph Fuller, tracked what actually happened after companies announced they were dropping degree requirements — not the press release, the hiring data. The finding: fewer than 1 in 700 hires last year actually went to someone without a degree who would have been screened out under the old rules. Announcing a policy and changing a hiring practice turned out to be almost entirely different things. Fuller's own summary: "measurable but pretty modest changes."

That gap is not evidence the model doesn't work. It's evidence of exactly the same pattern this book already traced in a different condition entirely — a country granting legal freedom of movement in 1957 and still watching actual relocation stay eight times lower than it should have been, because the legal change removed only one barrier out of several real ones. Here, the companies that treated "remove the degree requirement" as a genuine hiring-process overhaul, not a policy announcement, got a different result. Firms Fuller's study classifies as Skills-Based Hiring Leaders hired 18 percent more non-degreed workers on average, not 1 in 700. Non-degreed hires at those firms had a retention rate 10 percentage points higher than their degree-holding colleagues. Workers who moved into roles that used to require a degree saw an average 25 percent salary increase. Byrd is one data point inside that better number, not the average case — and the gap between his story and the 1-in-700 statistic is the entire argument for why this condition needs more than a policy change to actually work.

The United Nations' closest global target, Sustainable Development Goal 4.1, commits every country to universal free primary and secondary education completion by 2030. That target is real and worth tracking, but it measures something narrower than what this book means by "without gatekeepers." SDG 4.1 asks whether a child finishes school. It says nothing about what happens after — whether a credential earned, or not earned, then determines what work a person is allowed to do for the rest of their life, regardless of whether they can actually do it. Access to a classroom and freedom from a gatekeeper are different problems, and the existing global target only measures the first one.

This is, honestly, the weakest-assembled condition in this book. Housing had Finland's fifteen-year national proof. Food security had Brazil's natural experiment, gained and lost and regained. Currency had a documented historical rejection with a named cause. Education without gatekeepers has one well-corroborated individual story and one study measuring how rarely that story generalizes. No country has run credentialing reform at Finland's scale or duration. No research connects skills-based hiring to the migration or currency arguments in the rest of this book, the way remittances connected borders to hunger, or labor mobility connected currency to unemployment. That connective work — what a truly gatekeeper-free system of work and learning would need beyond company-by-company policy announcements — is not a footnote here. It is most of what remains to be built, for this condition more than any of the other four.
