# Two: One Currency, No Arbitrage

In the summer of 2015, a Greek retiree could withdraw sixty euros a day. Not sixty euros of spending money — sixty euros, period, from any account, for any purpose, capped by government order because the country's banks had run out of cash and the European Central Bank had stopped propping them up. Banks closed for three weeks. Lines formed at ATMs before dawn. Foreign transfers were frozen. A country inside one of the richest currency unions on Earth was, for a summer, functionally cut off from its own money.

That crisis didn't happen because Greeks stopped working. It happened because Greece had given up the one tool every country normally has to absorb a shock like this — control of its own currency — and joined a union that had granted the *legal* right to live and work anywhere within it decades earlier, in 1957, and still couldn't absorb the shock. The Opening chapter already traced the mechanism this exposes: when money can't flow to where the work is, people have to be free to flow there instead — and in the EU, they've been legally free to since before the euro existed. Legal freedom and actual behavior turned out to be two different things. This chapter is about what happened to actual people once that gap mattered. Youth unemployment had already peaked near 60% two years earlier, in 2013, and was still above 50% the January before the capital controls hit. By 2015 and 2016, roughly a third of the entire population was living at risk of poverty. And across the five years from 2009 to 2014, some 20,000 scientists — doctors, academics, engineers, the people any country can least afford to lose — left Greece for other parts of Europe. Not because a factory closed. Because a system built to share money without sharing an economy asked ordinary people to absorb a shock it had no other mechanism to absorb.

Greece is what happens when a currency union outruns the actual behavior it depends on, legal permission or not. But a shared global currency has never even gotten that far. It has been proposed, seriously, once — and rejected for reasons that had nothing to do with whether it would have worked.

In July 1944, at a hotel in Bretton Woods, New Hampshire, John Maynard Keynes proposed something close to what this book's second victory condition describes: a global clearing union issuing its own international currency, which he called the bancor. The design had a feature no national currency has ever had — it punished countries for running large trade *surpluses*, not just deficits, forcing the world's strongest economies to recycle their surpluses back into the system rather than hoard them. In 1944, the country that stood to be punished hardest by that rule was the United States, fresh out of a war that had left it holding most of the world's gold and manufacturing capacity. The American delegation, led by Treasury official Harry Dexter White, proposed a different plan: a fund built from a fixed pool of national currencies and gold, with no new global currency at all. Keynes's bancor was, in the words of economic historians who have studied the negotiation, dead on arrival. The system that emerged from Bretton Woods resembled White's plan, and the "reserve currency" that anchored the entire postwar financial order wasn't the bancor. It was the US dollar.

This is worth sitting with, because it's easy to assume a global currency has never existed because it's technically impossible. That isn't why. It's never existed at scale because, the one time it was seriously proposed by someone with the standing to be heard, the country best positioned to block it had every incentive to block it, and did. The obstacle in 1944 wasn't a gap in economic theory. It was a great power declining to be constrained by a system it wouldn't control. There is no evidence that obstacle has gone away in the eighty years since.

The bancor's closest living descendant is the International Monetary Fund's Special Drawing Right, created in 1967 — a "basket" unit of account built from the US dollar, euro, Chinese yuan, Japanese yen, and British pound, used by central banks to settle balances with each other. It is not a currency anyone earns a wage in, spends at a market, or holds in a personal account. It has drawn renewed reform interest since the 2008 financial crisis, with China and parts of the UN system pushing to expand its role and reduce the world's dependence on a single national currency. But every serious SDR reform proposal on record is a reserve-asset adjustment for central banks and governments, not a retail currency for a genuinely unified world economy. Eighty years after Bretton Woods, the technical apparatus for something like a real global currency exists in outline. The political will to let it replace a great power's currency does not.

Economists have a name for the conditions under which a shared currency actually works well: optimum currency area theory, developed across decades by Robert Mundell, Ronald McKinnon, and Peter Kenen. The theory identifies four conditions a group of economies need before sharing a currency is a good idea rather than a trap: deep trade integration between them, labor mobility so people can move to where the work is, business cycles that rise and fall together rather than independently, and enough fiscal coordination that a downturn in one region can be cushioned by the others. The Eurozone, when it launched, satisfied the first condition reasonably well and badly failed the second: in any given year, roughly eight times fewer Europeans relocate across an internal EU border for work than Americans relocate across a state line. Greece's crisis is optimum currency area theory's prediction arriving exactly on schedule.

What would it take to do this at the scale this book asks for — a single currency for the entire world, not a regional bloc? The honest answer is that nobody has designed it, and this book will not pretend otherwise. The available research answers a narrower, prior question: does a shared currency require freedom of movement to work, or can it be built currency-first, with the labor market to follow? The Eurozone tried the second order and paid for it in a decade of unemployment, a summer of sixty-euro withdrawal limits, and twenty thousand scientists who left in a five-year span. The sequencing implied by that evidence, and by optimum currency area theory itself, is that a shared global currency is not this book's first victory condition to pursue. It is closer to the last one — the condition hardest to build safely until the others, especially free movement of people, are substantially already in place.
